Abstract

The Most Important Data Point in Real Estate Nobody Is Showing You

The United States residential real estate market contains 158 million properties worth approximately $47 trillion. Every transaction in this market is made with incomplete information.

The Multiple Listing Service — the foundational data infrastructure of American real estate — was built for a world of stable insurance markets and predictable climate risk. That world is changing faster than the MLS can track.

DEED is a real-time property intelligence platform being built around one specific insight that no existing platform has acted on:

When insurance carriers withdraw from a county they send the most accurate signal available about that property market's future value — months before any map, model, or Zestimate reflects it. That data is publicly available in state insurance commissioner filings across all 50 states. Nobody is aggregating it in real time into a property-level intelligence score. DEED does.

DEED scores every property in America — listed or not — and shows buyers the true monthly payment their bank will actually collect, not the standard estimate that can be $1,000 or more per month wrong.

The $DEED token on Base funds the data infrastructure that makes this possible through a 1% transaction fee that pays for API costs at $0.01 per property search, with surplus invested into yield-bearing assets on Base to compound the treasury over time.

Section 01

The Problem

1.1 The Insurance Withdrawal Crisis

35.6 million US properties currently face escalating insurance costs and reduced coverage due to climate risk. Major carriers — State Farm, Allstate, Farmers, Nationwide — have withdrawn from entire counties across Florida, California, Louisiana, Texas, and North Carolina.

The Florida FAIR Plan — the state's insurer of last resort — tripled in enrollment from 140,000 policies in 2018 to over 610,000 by mid-2025. Average Florida home insurance rates reached $7,562 per year — the highest in the nation.

This information exists in public records. Every state insurance commissioner publishes carrier withdrawal filings. The data is available to anyone willing to aggregate it across all 50 states. Nobody is doing this in real time at the property level. That is the gap DEED is built to fill.

1.2 The True PITI Problem

When a buyer finances a home purchase the lender requires insurance and collects it through escrow. Insurance is baked into the monthly payment. The total payment — Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance — is called PITI.

Zillow's payment calculator uses a standard insurance estimate regardless of local market conditions. In insurance-stressed markets this estimate can be $1,000 or more per month below the actual market rate.

A real example from Q1 2026 — Collier County, Florida:

Zillow standard insurance estimate: $153/month
Actual FAIR Plan premium: $1,533/month
Difference: $1,380/month

Zillow estimated PITI: $4,967/month
Real PITI: $6,347/month

Result: Buyer disqualified from mortgage based on debt-to-income ratio — discovered at closing, not before making the offer.

This is not an edge case. It is the current reality in dozens of counties across America and the number is growing every month. DEED shows the real number before the offer is made.

1.3 The Valuation Gap

Zillow's Zestimate is calculated from recent comparable sales. It is backward-looking — it reflects what properties sold for, not what they are worth given current insurance market conditions.

A property in a county where two major carriers withdrew in the past six months is not worth what a comparable property sold for before those withdrawals. The Zestimate has no mechanism to price insurance market deterioration. DEED does.

1.4 The Off-Market Gap

Active MLS listings represent approximately 1-2 million properties at any given time. There are 158 million residential properties in America. Every homeowner — not just those currently selling — has a stake in understanding their property's insurance situation.

DEED scores every property in America whether it is listed for sale or not. A homeowner in a county where carriers are quietly withdrawing deserves to know before their renewal notice arrives with a 300% premium increase.

3.2 The Data Pipeline — Build Order

Phase 1 — Property Records

BatchData API integration. 158 million US properties. Tax assessor records, ownership history, transaction data. Cost: $0.01 per API call. Free developer tier available for initial testing and development.

Phase 2 — Climate Risk

First Street Foundation API. Flood, fire, heat, wind, and air quality risk scores for every US address. Free developer tier available. Production tier approximately $200/month at scale.

Phase 3 — Insurance Withdrawal Database

This is the most important and most labor-intensive component. State insurance commissioner filings across all 50 states are public but fragmented — each state uses different formats, different filing systems, and different update schedules.

Built in two stages. Stage 1: manual aggregation of the five highest-priority states — Florida, California, Texas, Louisiana, North Carolina. Stage 2: automated scrapers for all 50 states normalized into a standardized format that feeds the DEED score engine automatically.

This database is DEED's primary competitive moat. It grows more accurate and comprehensive over time and cannot be quickly replicated by a well-funded competitor — because the data must be accumulated over time, verified against agent ground truth, and maintained continuously.

Phase 4 — Agent Verification Layer

Licensed real estate agents submit hyperlocal insurance intelligence through the agent portal — informal carrier tightening before any filing appears, actual quote availability, local market conditions the data cannot capture. This ground truth layer makes DEED's insurance scores more accurate than any data source alone.

4.5 The Treasury Yield Model

4.6 The Deflationary Burn Mechanism

We considered not including the burn mechanism because it sounds almost too good. We're including it because it's honest and because it creates the most direct possible alignment between platform accuracy and token value. Supply only decreases when the platform earns it.

As the treasury grows through fees and yield, surplus funds are used to buy $DEED on the open market and burn permanently. Every burn is announced publicly, executed on-chain, and verifiable by anyone on BaseScan.

Burns are triggered when:

— Treasury surplus exceeds 90 days of operational reserves
— A significant platform milestone is reached
— $DEED holders vote to trigger a burn event

Every burn reduces total supply permanently. The 210,000,000 cap only decreases. Never increases.

The compounding cycle: More $DEED trading ↓ More 1% fees collected ↓ More surplus above operational costs ↓ More deployed into yield on Base (4-8% APY) ↓ More interest earned ↓ More operational costs covered by yield ↓ More surplus available for burns ↓ More $DEED burned permanently ↓ Scarcer supply · each token worth more ↓ More $DEED trading

Section 08

The Honest Risks

Data Pipeline Risk: The insurance withdrawal database does not exist yet. Building it requires developer time, ongoing maintenance, and manual effort in early stages. Coverage will be incomplete at launch and improve over time.
Developer Risk: DEED currently has no developer. The technical build depends on finding the right person willing to work for $DEED tokens in the early stage. This is the most critical dependency in the entire project.
Token Liquidity Risk: $DEED will launch with limited initial liquidity — approximately $50–100 of ETH paired with $DEED on Aerodrome. Early token price will be volatile. Thin liquidity means large buys and sells move the price significantly.
Regulatory Risk: Real estate data, token offerings, and AI-generated analysis operate in a rapidly evolving regulatory environment. DEED is not a licensed real estate broker, financial advisor, or investment advisor. DEED presents data. Users make their own decisions.
Competition Risk: Zillow, CoStar, or a well-funded PropTech startup could build similar features. DEED's moat is the insurance withdrawal database accumulated over time and the agent network that verifies hyperlocal data. Both take time to build but neither is impossible to replicate given sufficient resources.
Founder Risk: DEED is currently a one-person founding team. This is both a strength — complete clarity of vision and principle — and a risk. The developer and early community members who join reduce this risk substantially.
